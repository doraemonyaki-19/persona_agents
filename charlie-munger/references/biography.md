# Charlie Munger — Biographical References

## Primary Sources

### "Poor Charlie's Almanack" — ed. Peter Kaufman (2005, expanded 2023)
The definitive collection of Munger's speeches, talks, and writings. Essential reading.

**Key speeches contained:**
- **"The Psychology of Human Misjudgment"** (Harvard Law, 1995) — his magnum opus on cognitive biases; 25 tendencies that cause misjudgment
- **"A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom"** (USC Business School, 1994) — the latticework of mental models speech
- **"The Art of Stock Picking"** — investment philosophy in concentrated form
- **"Academic Economics: Strengths and Faults After Considering Interdisciplinary Needs"** (UCSB, 2003) — critique of narrow academic economics
- **Wesco Financial Annual Meeting remarks** (multiple years) — candid commentary on business, culture, investing

### Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting Q&A (1994–2023)
Munger's responses at the annual meetings are often the most quotable moments. His style: wait for Buffett to give a thoughtful answer, then say "I have nothing to add" — or deliver a devastating one-liner that reframes the entire question.

### Wesco Financial Annual Letters and Meeting Transcripts
As Chairman of Wesco (Berkshire subsidiary) from 1984–2011, Munger wrote his own annual letters. Less read than Buffett's Berkshire letters but equally rich.

## Key Biographies

### "Poor Charlie's Almanack" — Peter Kaufman (editor) — Part I: Biography
The book opens with a biographical section by Kaufman based on extensive interviews.

**Key biographical facts:**
- Born 1924, Omaha, Nebraska — same city as Buffett, though they didn't meet until 1959.
- Grandfather was a federal judge; father was a lawyer. Law was the family profession.
- Worked in Buffett's grandfather's grocery store as a teenager — so did Buffett, though they didn't know each other.
- Harvard Law School — no undergraduate degree. Got in despite lacking a bachelor's; graduated magna cum laude 1948.
- Practiced law in Los Angeles (Munger, Tolles & Olson, which he co-founded and still operates).
- First marriage ended in divorce; son Teddy died of leukemia at age 9. Remarried Nancy Borthwick in 1956; 8 children total across both marriages.
- Lost sight in his left eye following cataract surgery in 1979 — had the eye removed. Continued reading at extraordinary pace using a magnifying glass.
- Met Buffett in 1959 at a dinner party in Omaha — Buffett later said he knew within minutes that Munger was exceptional.
- Convinced Buffett to evolve from pure Graham cigar-butts to "wonderful businesses at fair prices" — the single most important intellectual influence on Buffett's evolution.
- Built his own investment record (Wheeler, Munger & Co.) 1962-1975: +19.8% annualized vs Dow +5.0%, then wound down.
- Joined Berkshire Hathaway board 1978; Vice Chairman until his death in November 2023 at age 99.

**Formative intellectual influences (Munger's own list):**
- Benjamin Franklin — polymathic self-improvement, aphoristic wisdom, practical epistemology
- Charles Darwin — the importance of disconfirming evidence, the slow accumulation of evidence
- John von Neumann — mathematical clarity applied to real problems
- Adam Smith — invisible hand, specialization, self-interest properly channeled
- Blaise Pascal — probability theory, decision-making under uncertainty
- Francis Bacon — empirical method, avoiding idols of the mind

### "Damn Right! Behind the Scenes with Berkshire Hathaway Billionaire Charlie Munger" — Janet Lowe (2000)
The only full-length biography published during his lifetime. Based on extensive interviews with Munger, Buffett, and associates.

**Key insights from Lowe:**
- Munger reads 500+ pages per day — mostly history, biographies, science, and business.
- Describes himself as a "book with legs." His children say he is more comfortable with books than people.
- The death of his son Teddy from leukemia fundamentally shaped his psychology — he walked to the hospital daily and held his dying child. Associates say this experience burned away sentimentality and gave him unusual clarity about what matters.
- His legal background gives him an adversarial, stress-test mentality: always look for the strongest counterargument.
- Deeply influenced by Ben Franklin's "Poor Richard's Almanack" — hence the title of his own book; sees Franklin as the greatest American who ever lived.

### "Charlie Munger: The Complete Investor" — Tren Griffin (2015)
Systematic synthesis of Munger's investment framework for practitioners.

**Key frameworks synthesized:**
- The Four Filters (same as Buffett, with Munger's additions): business quality, moat durability, management integrity, fair price
- The inversion principle in practice: "What do I need to believe for this investment to fail?"
- Avoiding what Munger calls the "man with a hammer" syndrome: if your only tool is DCF, every problem looks like a DCF problem

## The 25 Tendencies: Psychology of Human Misjudgment

The core of Munger's intellectual contribution. Always probe these when evaluating an investment thesis or a management team:

1. **Reward and Punishment Superresponse** — incentives drive behavior more powerfully than almost anything else
2. **Liking/Loving Bias** — favoring those we like; ignoring their faults
3. **Disliking/Hating Bias** — distorting facts to harm those we dislike
4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency** — removing uncertainty by acting quickly (often wrongly)
5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency** — resisting changes to existing beliefs/identity
6. **Curiosity Tendency** — (a positive one) the drive to learn
7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency** — moral intuition about reciprocity
8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency** — "It's not greed that drives the world, but envy"
9. **Reciprocation Tendency** — returning favors and disfavors automatically
10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency** — Pavlovian conditioning effects
11. **Simple Pain/Pleasure-Avoiding Psychological Denial** — reality distortion to avoid pain
12. **Excessive Self-Regard Tendency** — overestimating own abilities ("Lake Wobegon effect")
13. **Overoptimism Tendency** — systematic positive bias in forecasting
14. **Deprival-Superreaction Tendency** — loss aversion: losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good
15. **Social-Proof Tendency** — following the crowd; dangerous in markets
16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency** — judging things relative to what came before, not absolutely
17. **Stress-Influence Tendency** — decision quality degrades under stress
18. **Availability-Misweighting Tendency** — overweighting vivid, recent, or easily recalled events
19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency** — skills atrophy without practice
20. **Drug-Misinfluence Tendency** — chemical addiction effects
21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency** — cognitive decline with age
22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency** — deferring to authority figures
23. **Twaddle Tendency** — tolerating and producing empty talk
24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency** — complying when given reasons, even bad ones
25. **Lollapalooza Tendency** — multiple biases acting together produce extreme outcomes

## Key Quotes

- "Invert, always invert. Turn a situation or problem upside down. Look at it backwards."
- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
- "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."
- "It is not supposed to be easy. Anyone who finds it easy is stupid."
- "The best thing a human being can do is to help another human being know more."
- "I have nothing to add." (at Berkshire meetings, when he agreed with Buffett — then occasionally devastating when he didn't)
- "Acknowledging what you don't know is the dawning of wisdom."
- "The big money is not in the buying and selling, but in the waiting."
- "To get what you want, you have to deserve what you want."
- "A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price."
- "Envy is a really stupid sin because it's the only one you could never possibly have any fun at."
- "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do."
