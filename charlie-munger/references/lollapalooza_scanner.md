# Lollapalooza Scanner

Munger: "When I see a situation where multiple psychological tendencies are acting simultaneously in the same direction, I expect extreme results — good or bad."

A lollapalooza effect occurs when 3 or more forces (biases, incentives, social dynamics, competitive pressures) all point in the same direction. Outcomes are non-linear: not additive but multiplicative. This creates both the greatest investment opportunities (underpriced lollapaloozas) and the greatest dangers (overpriced ones; frauds; bubbles).

## Part 2: Classic Lollapalooza Patterns

### Pattern A: The Bubble (Bullish Lollapalooza — Danger)

Forces that converge to create unsustainable asset inflation:

- [ ] **Social proof**: everyone is buying; returns are front-page news
- [ ] **FOMO / envy**: investors feel left out and chase performance
- [ ] **Availability bias**: recent strong returns seem like the new normal
- [ ] **Incentive superresponse**: brokers, bankers, fund managers all paid on volume/AUM
- [ ] **Authority figures**: credible people validating the thesis publicly
- [ ] **Overoptimism**: projections assume current trends continue indefinitely
- [ ] **Doubt-avoidance**: bears are mocked; doubt is socially punished
- [ ] **Reciprocation**: favorable coverage from those being paid by the industry

**Bubble Checklist Count:** ___/8

If ≥ 5: This is a textbook bubble formation. The question is only timing.
Munger's action: reduce exposure; prepare for the correction; be willing to look early and wrong.

---

### Pattern B: The Fraud / Corporate Failure (Bearish Lollapalooza — Danger for longs)

Forces that converge to create corporate collapse:

- [ ] **Incentive misalignment**: executives rewarded for revenue/stock price, not FCF or durability
- [ ] **Commitment bias**: management has publicly committed to targets they cannot meet
- [ ] **Denial**: obvious problems are being explained away in investor communications
- [ ] **Social proof in reverse**: peers doing the same thing ("everyone does it")
- [ ] **Overoptimism in projections**: guidance consistently optimistic vs. actuals
- [ ] **Authority reliance**: investors defer to the CEO's charisma rather than the numbers
- [ ] **Complexity as camouflage**: accounting or business structure too complex to audit easily
- [ ] **Leverage**: balance sheet amplifies any negative shock

**Fraud/Failure Risk Count:** ___/8

If ≥ 5: Elevated risk of serious negative outcome. Requires deeper forensic work or avoidance.

---

### Pattern C: The Deep Value Lollapalooza (Bullish Opportunity — Rare)

Forces that converge to create extreme undervaluation:

- [ ] **Forced selling**: index removal, ETF rebalancing, margin calls, fund liquidation
- [ ] **Availability bias**: recent bad news makes the sector/stock seem perpetually broken
- [ ] **Hatred/disliking bias**: the company/industry is genuinely disliked for non-economic reasons
- [ ] **Social proof in reverse**: "smart money" is avoiding this; being long is embarrassing
- [ ] **Regulatory fear**: overhang from a regulatory event that is actually resolvable
- [ ] **Short-term pain hiding long-term value**: restructuring charges masking earning power
- [ ] **Capital structure complexity**: obscures the equity value beneath debt layers

**Deep Value Lollapalooza Count:** ___/7

If ≥ 4: May be a genuine Munger-style opportunity. Requires thorough fundamental work to confirm the convergence is temporary.

---

### Pattern D: The Competitive Moat Reinforcement (Compound Lollapalooza — Great Business)

Forces that converge to make a strong business stronger over time:

- [ ] **Network effects** increasing in strength
- [ ] **Switching costs** deepening as integration increases
- [ ] **Brand reinforcement**: each positive customer experience strengthens the brand
- [ ] **Scale advantages**: growth reduces unit economics, widening cost gap
- [ ] **Regulatory moat**: operating licenses/approvals create natural barriers
- [ ] **Winner-take-most dynamics**: market concentrating toward the leader
- [ ] **Talent flywheel**: best-in-class employees attract more best-in-class employees

**Moat Reinforcement Count:** ___/7

If ≥ 5: The business is likely to be much stronger in 10 years than today. Justifies a premium valuation.

## Part 3: Lollapalooza in the Analyst's Own Process

Munger's most important warning: lollapalooza effects operate on the analyst, not just the market.

**Am I experiencing a personal lollapalooza?**

Forces potentially converging on my analysis:
- [ ] I like the management (liking bias)
- [ ] I've publicly committed to a view (consistency bias)
- [ ] Others I respect own this (social proof)
- [ ] I recently made money on a similar thesis (availability bias)
- [ ] I'm under pressure to have a position (stress)
- [ ] I'm paid to have a positive view (incentive superresponse)

**My personal bias convergence count:** ___

If ≥ 3: My analysis requires an independent, adversarial review before acting.

## Part 4: Historical Lollapalooza Examples

Use as calibration references:

| Event | Forces Converged | Outcome |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **2000 dot-com bubble** | Social proof, FOMO, overoptimism, incentive misalignment (IPO fees), availability bias (tech returns), authority (Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" ignored) | Nasdaq −78% |
| **2008 subprime** | Incentive misalignment (originate-to-distribute), complexity camouflage (CDOs), authority (AAA ratings), overoptimism (housing prices never fall), social proof (everyone doing it), leverage | Global financial crisis |
| **Enron (2001)** | Incentive misalignment, complexity, commitment bias (analysts who covered it), authority (Arthur Andersen), denial by management, excessive leverage | Bankruptcy, prison |
| **Berkshire buying See's Candies (1972)** | Forced seller (family estate), availability bias (manufacturing companies valued on book), sector hatred (consumer goods unfashionable), brand underappreciation | Munger/Buffett's best investment |
| **Tobacco stocks 1990s** | Sector hatred (ethical), regulatory fear (overhang), social proof (everyone avoiding), availability (cancer lawsuits) | 10x returns for patient holders |
