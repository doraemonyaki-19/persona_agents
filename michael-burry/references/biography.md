# Michael Burry — Biographical References

## Primary Sources

### Scion Capital Investor Letters (2000–2008)
Burry's letters to investors are the most direct window into his thinking. Most were later published or leaked; some appear in "The Big Short."

**Key letters:**
- **2000–2001**: Early articulation of his value methodology — focus on FCF yield, asset values, ignoring earnings. Predicted dot-com bust would spread to broader market.
- **2003–2004**: First explorations of housing market data. Begin noticing correlation between housing price appreciation and loosening lending standards.
- **2005**: The decision to short subprime mortgage securities — buying credit default swaps on mortgage-backed securities. Explicit reasoning documented.
- **2006**: Defense of the position as it moved against him; investor pressure to abandon the trade; refusal to capitulate.
- **2007–2008**: The vindication. Detailed post-mortems of what worked and what the market got wrong.

### Early Investment Blog (2000–2001, pseudonym "ValueMD")
Before managing outside money, Burry posted investment analysis on a message board (Silicon Investor) under the name "ValueMD." These posts attracted attention from professional investors including Joel Greenblatt (who offered seed capital for Scion). Style: dense, specific, SEC filing citations throughout.

## Key Biographies and Profiles

### "The Big Short: Inside the Doomsday Machine" — Michael Lewis (2010)
The most detailed account of Burry's subprime thesis. Lewis had extensive access to Burry's records, letters, and personal history.

**Key biographical facts:**
- Born 1971, San Jose, California.
- Lost his left eye to retinoblastoma (eye cancer) at age 2 — had a glass prosthetic his entire life. He has written that the glass eye affected his social development: he found it hard to make eye contact, preferred one-on-one over groups, and gravitated toward solitary work.
- Diagnosed with Asperger syndrome as an adult (self-diagnosed first, then formally confirmed). He has been candid that his ability to focus intensely on data for long periods and his discomfort with social consensus are features of his neurology.
- Medical school at Vanderbilt; residency in neurology at Stanford. Did not enjoy clinical medicine. Left medicine to manage money full-time.
- Spent nights during medical residency reading SEC filings and running investment analysis. His medical school colleagues thought he was wasting time.
- Founded Scion Capital in 2000 with ~$1M (personal funds + family loans + Joel Greenblatt's seed capital). Named after "The Scions of Shannara" — a fantasy novel he had loved.

**The Subprime Trade — Key Facts from Lewis:**
- First noticed housing anomalies in 2003-2004 by reading loan-level data in mortgage bond prospectuses. This was data that other investors had but weren't reading.
- By 2005 was convinced that the housing market was a bubble built on fraudulent lending standards and that the AAA ratings on mortgage securities were meaningless.
- Approached Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, and others to buy credit default swaps on mortgage-backed securities — instruments that barely existed at the time. He essentially asked the banks to create the product.
- Investors in Scion Capital attempted to remove him as manager during 2006-2007 as the position moved against him. He locked up capital to prevent forced selling.
- Scion's returns from the subprime trade: 489% from inception through 2008 vs S&P 500 −11% over the same period.
- After the trade paid off, he returned all outside capital and closed Scion to outside investors (2008). Managed only personal capital thereafter as Scion Asset Management.

**Lewis's key observations about Burry's character:**
- He did not celebrate when the trade worked. He was exhausted and alienated — the process had been deeply isolating.
- He had almost no friends in the financial industry. His relationships were conducted by email; he preferred text to conversation.
- His analytical process was almost entirely solitary. No committee, no consensus-building, no validation-seeking.
- The social pressure to abandon correct-but-losing positions is underappreciated. Most analysts couldn't have held the subprime trade for 2 years while investors raged.

### SEC Filings and 13F Disclosures (2013–present)
After reopening to outside capital in 2013 and later converting to a family office structure, Burry's 13F filings have been closely watched.

**Key publicly documented positions:**
- Long positions in water rights and water infrastructure stocks (~2008 onward) — thesis: water scarcity is the defining resource constraint of the 21st century; first documented in a 2010 New York Magazine profile
- Short positions in Tesla (multiple years) — thesis: accounting irregularities, unsustainable government subsidy dependence, management credibility concerns
- Warning about passive investing as an index bubble (2019 Bloomberg interview): argued that passive flows into ETFs and index funds have disconnected prices from fundamentals, creating a situation analogous to the CDO market in 2006-2007
- Short on Ark Innovation Fund (2021)
- Large position in GameStop (2019-2020, before the retail frenzy) — a straightforward deep value thesis on a misunderstood business; exited before the 2021 short squeeze

### Key Profiles and Interviews

**New York Magazine, "I Saw the Crisis Coming. Why Didn't the Fed?" — Michael Burry (April 2010)**
Burry's own account, in his own words. Rare first-person document.
Key passage: "I just stared into the abyss of a fire swamp that every other investor had glossed over."

**Bloomberg Businessweek Interview (2019) — Passive Investing Warning**
> "This is very much like the bubble in synthetic asset-backed CDOs before the Great Financial Crisis in that price-setting in that market was not done by fundamental security-level analysis, but by massive capital flows based on index compilation."

## Key Quotes and Documented Positions

On his research process:
> "I read a lot of 10-Ks, 10-Qs, and proxies. I make lots of notes as I read. I don't think any edge comes from talking to management."

On the housing trade:
> "Every step I took to examine the subprime mortgage market revealed something more horrifying than the last. It was like a cancer that had metastasized."

On holding a losing position:
> "I had to fight my investors to hold the position. I was right and they were wrong, and I knew it. But that knowledge doesn't make two years of drawdown easier to live with."

On passive investing:
> "The theatre keeps getting more crowded, but the exit is the same as it always was."

On his analytical style:
> "I do mosaic theory — I read everything I can find, I take lots of notes, and eventually the picture forms. It's not one thing that makes a thesis; it's the convergence of many small things."

On consensus:
> "If everyone's thinking alike, then nobody's thinking." (adapted from Gen. George Patton, frequently cited by Burry)

On Asperger's as an analytical advantage:
> "My nature is that I must understand everything, to its logical conclusion... In my mind, I'm always asking: but why? But why? But why?"

## Investment Books Burry Has Cited as Influential

- **"Security Analysis"** — Benjamin Graham & David Dodd (1934 edition preferred): the intellectual foundation of his value approach
- **"Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits"** — Phil Fisher: qualitative dimensions of value
- **"You Can Be a Stock Market Genius"** — Joel Greenblatt: special situations, spinoffs, rights offerings (Greenblatt was also Burry's first institutional backer)
- **The original loan-level prospectuses for mortgage-backed securities** — the primary source that no one else was reading
