# Warren Buffett — Biographical References

## Primary Sources

### Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters (1965–present)
The single most important primary source. Buffett writes every letter himself, no PR polish.
Key letters to draw from:
- **1977**: First articulation of "wonderful business at a fair price" vs. cigar butts
- **1983**: The See's Candies lesson — pricing power and intangible value
- **1987**: Introduction of the "economic moat" concept
- **1989**: "The Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years" — brutal self-assessment
- **1992**: Intrinsic value, book value, and owner earnings defined
- **1996**: Mr. Market allegory (from Graham), circle of competence articulated
- **2001**: 9/11 letter — how to think about catastrophic risk
- **2007**: Warning on derivatives as "financial weapons of mass destruction"
- **2014**: The Nebraska farm analogy for long-term thinking
- **2016**: Defense of American capitalism, productivity, and compounding

### "The Essays of Warren Buffett" (ed. Lawrence Cunningham, multiple editions)
Cunningham reorganized the letters by theme. Essential reading for:
- Corporate governance and owner-oriented principles
- Accounting and valuation methodology
- Investment philosophy and portfolio management
- Mergers, acquisitions, and capital allocation

## Key Biographies

### "The Snowball: Warren Buffett and the Business of Life" — Alice Schroeder (2008)
The authorized biography. 960 pages based on hundreds of hours of interviews.

**Key biographical facts:**
- Born 1930, Omaha, Nebraska. Father Howard Buffett was a stockbroker and Congressman.
- Bought his first stock (Cities Service preferred) at age 11 with his sister Doris.
- Filed his first tax return at age 13, deducting his bicycle as a business expense (paper route).
- Read "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham at 19 — described it as "the best book about investing ever written." Rewrote his entire investment philosophy after reading it.
- Rejected from Harvard Business School. Enrolled at Columbia to study under Graham.
- Worked at Graham-Newman Corp 1954-1956. Graham was the only employer he ever had.
- Started Buffett Partnership Ltd. in 1956 with $105,100 (including $100 of his own).
- Wound down the partnership in 1969, returned capital, citing inability to find value in a frothy market.
- Took control of Berkshire Hathaway (originally a textile mill) — later called it the "dumbest stock I ever bought" because it was a cigar butt, not a wonderful business.
- Bought See's Candies in 1972 for $25M — transformative acquisition that taught him to pay for quality.
- Married Susan Thompson in 1952; she moved to San Francisco in 1977 while Buffett remained in Omaha with Astrid Menks (whom he married after Susan's death in 2004).
- Lives in the same house he bought in 1958 for $31,500.

**Key formative experiences:**
- Ben Graham's "Mr. Market" and "margin of safety" concepts: the intellectual foundation
- Phil Fisher's "Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits": introduced the qualitative dimension (management quality, competitive position)
- Charlie Munger's influence (from ~1959 onward): pushed Buffett from cigar butts to quality businesses
- The See's Candies acquisition: proved that intangible value (brand, customer loyalty, pricing power) was real and compoundable
- The Salomon Brothers crisis (1991): nearly destroyed Berkshire's reputation; Buffett took CEO role personally to save it — reinforced the importance of reputation management

**Schroeder's key observations:**
- Buffett's emotional detachment from money is genuine: it's always been a scorecard, never a consumption good
- His risk aversion is deep and personal — born from watching his father's investment clients suffer losses
- The Omaha insularity is strategic: distance from Wall Street noise is a competitive advantage
- His greatest regret: not buying more of the companies he understood early (Walmart, etc.)

### "Warren Buffett Speaks" — Janet Lowe (1997, updated 2007)
Compilation of quotes and interviews. Useful for characteristic phrasing and one-liners.

**Selected key quotes:**
- "Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1."
- "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."
- "Our favorite holding period is forever."
- "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful."
- "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."
- "In the short run, the market is a voting machine. In the long run, it is a weighing machine." (Graham, frequently cited by Buffett)
- "I never attempt to make money on the stock market. I buy on the assumption that they could close the market the next day and not reopen it for five years."
- "Wide diversification is only required when investors do not understand what they are doing."
- "Risk comes from not knowing what you're doing."
- "Time is the friend of the wonderful company, the enemy of the mediocre."
- "Someone's sitting in the shade today because someone planted a tree a long time ago."
- "The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient."
- "It takes 20 years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it."

## Key Investment Frameworks (From Letters + Interviews)

### Owner Earnings (1986 Letter)
> "Owner earnings = reported earnings + depreciation/depletion/amortization − average annual capex required to maintain competitive position − any additional working capital required"

The single best measure of what a business actually earns for its owners, as distinct from accounting earnings.

### The Four Filters (for acquisitions)
1. Businesses we can understand
2. Favorable long-term economics (durable competitive advantage)
3. Able and trustworthy management
4. Sensible price

### On Diversification
> "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you are doing."
Buffett ran concentrated portfolios his whole career. Top 5 positions often >60% of portfolio.

### On Macro Forecasting
> "We've long felt that the only value of stock forecasters is to make fortune tellers look good... Charlie and I have not the slightest idea what the stock market will do next week, next month, or next year."

### On Leverage
Berkshire never operates with significant debt at the holding company level. The lesson from Long-Term Capital Management (1998): "To finish first, you must first finish."

### On Technology
Buffett avoided technology stocks for decades — not because he thought they were bad businesses, but because he couldn't reliably predict which companies would have durable moats. The exception was Apple (bought 2016-2018), which he analyzed as a consumer products company with extraordinary switching costs, not a technology company.
